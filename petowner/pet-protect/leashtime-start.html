The Basics of Pet Law

Dog Bite Liability is governed by state laws, however, the determination of liability for a dog bite incident can vary dramatically from one state to another. For instance, if a dog bites your neighbor after they came onto your lawn in Virginia and it is the first time the dog has bitten anyone, you likely will have absolutely zero liability. The same incident in California would hold you personally liable. Significant elements of each state's law are often determined by laws implemented in your particular municipality. Further, some legal issues pertaining to pets are covered by Federal law (e.g. Americans with Disabilities Act for service designated dogs). It can intimidating and confusing to find the relevant legal information, much less interpret that information. Whether your dog has bitten someone or you have been bitten by a dog, the consequences and outcomes can vary greatly depending on the state and local jurisdiction where you reside. There are three main classifications of legal liability with regard to dog bites:

  • Contributory negligence: if the victim has even 1% responsibilty for the dog bite incident, then dog bite law dictates that they cannot recover damages. Also known as the "one-bite" rule

  • Pure comparative falut: apportions damages for dog bite incident based on the relative fault of the victim

  • 51% bar: if the victim is less than 51% at fault for the dog bite incident, then they are wholly absolved from any contributory negligence and can recover full civil damages
